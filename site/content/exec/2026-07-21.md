## The Week at a Glance

- 🔴 **High — Exchange 2016/2019 support ends in October 2026, with no further extensions.** Organizations still running on-premises Exchange have roughly 90 days to migrate or face unpatched, unsupported email infrastructure.
- 🔴 **High — Active credential-theft campaigns (ACR Stealer) are targeting enterprise browsers right now.** Attackers are harvesting saved passwords and authentication tokens from employee devices using deceptive prompts. This is an active, confirmed threat against organizations like yours.
- 🟡 **Medium — Post-quantum encryption protections are now available in Windows 11 and Server 2025.** This is a staged transition that requires planning; organizations in regulated industries or with long-lived sensitive data should begin evaluating readiness.
- 🟢 **Low — New Zero Trust controls for AI, web, and private app access are entering preview.** Microsoft is extending identity-based access verification to AI agents and cloud traffic; worth tracking as AI adoption grows.

## Why This Week Matters

The combination of an expiring support deadline for on-premises Exchange and an active, confirmed wave of browser credential theft creates compounding risk for organizations that delay action on either front. Stolen authentication tokens can bypass multi-factor authentication entirely, meaning even well-protected organizations are exposed if employee devices are compromised. The one thing leadership must understand this week: credential theft via browser is not a theoretical future threat; Microsoft's own security researchers have confirmed it is succeeding in enterprise environments right now, and the stolen tokens can unlock access as if the attacker were a legitimate employee.

## What Your Employees Will Notice

- **No immediate visible changes for most users this week.** The security updates and infrastructure changes described above are applied by IT administrators and do not produce user-facing prompts or workflow changes.
- **Employees using Windows 11 with the July update installed will benefit from stronger encryption on their network connections automatically**, with no action or awareness required on their part.
- **Employees should be reminded now to avoid clicking unexpected prompts in their browser** that ask them to paste text into a command window or take unusual steps to "verify" their identity. The ACR Stealer campaigns use this technique, called ClickFix, to trick employees into executing malicious code. This is a timely topic for a brief security reminder to all staff.
- **Copilot users in GCC High and DoD environments** will now have access to declarative agents that can take actions, such as calling external systems and updating data, directly from the Copilot chat interface. Governance policies for these capabilities should be reviewed before broad employee use.

## What Your Help Desk Should Expect

- **Increased phishing and social engineering reports.** Given active ACR Stealer campaigns, employees who receive unusual browser prompts or notice unexpected account activity may contact the help desk. Ensure front-line staff know to escalate these reports promptly rather than treat them as routine phishing attempts, as stolen tokens require a different response than a clicked phishing link.
- **Questions about Exchange migration timelines.** IT staff and business unit leaders who become aware of the October deadline may ask the help desk or IT leadership about migration plans and timelines. A prepared, clear answer from leadership reduces anxiety and rumor.
- **Post-quantum update questions from technically aware staff.** The Windows 11 July update includes visible changes to encryption capabilities. Some employees may ask what this means for them; the answer is that it improves the security of their connections automatically with no action needed.
- **GCC High and DoD environments — Copilot agent capability questions.** Users in these environments now have access to Copilot agents that can take real-world actions. Help desk teams may receive questions about what these agents can access and how they are controlled.

## Cost & Licensing

- **Exchange migration carries real budget implications.** Organizations still running Exchange 2016 or 2019 on-premises face a decision between migrating to Exchange Online (a recurring licensing cost, typically included in Microsoft 365 business or enterprise plans), upgrading to Exchange Server Subscription Edition (a perpetual license requiring hardware and maintenance), or a hybrid approach. Delaying this decision does not defer the cost; it increases risk while the eventual migration cost remains. Budget owners should request a migration cost estimate immediately.
- **Purview Data Lifecycle Management archiving (coming August 2026 preview) may reduce storage costs.** The upcoming feature that automatically moves inactive OneDrive and SharePoint files to lower-cost storage tiers could meaningfully reduce storage spend for organizations with large content repositories, while maintaining compliance and Copilot search quality. This is worth flagging to whoever manages M365 storage and licensing budgets.
- **Microsoft Entra Internet Access and Private Access (Zero Trust Network Access) are separate licensed products.** Organizations interested in the expanded Zero Trust controls for AI agents and remote access announced this week should confirm with IT whether these products are included in their current agreement or would represent new spend.

## If You Take No Action

**Exchange 2016/2019 reaches end of support with no migration plan in place:** After October 2026, every new vulnerability discovered in Exchange 2016 or 2019 will remain permanently unpatched. Your organization's email infrastructure becomes an increasing liability over time. Many cyber insurance carriers require that supported, patched software be in use; running unpatched Exchange may void coverage for email-related incidents. Regulatory frameworks including HIPAA and CMMC treat known unpatched vulnerabilities as compliance findings that can result in audit failures, fines, or loss of certification.

**ACR Stealer campaigns go unaddressed:** If no action is taken to harden endpoint browser security or alert employees, the probability of a successful credential or token theft event increases with each passing week. A stolen authentication token gives an attacker the same access as a legitimate employee, often without triggering standard MFA alerts. The downstream consequences include unauthorized data access, ransomware deployment from a trusted account, and the breach notification obligations that follow under HIPAA, GDPR, and applicable state privacy laws.
